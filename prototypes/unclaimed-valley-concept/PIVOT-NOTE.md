# PIVOT NOTE — The Unclaimed Valley (discovery slice)

> **Date:** 2026-06-26
> **Verdict:** PIVOT
> Read this before running the next discovery prototype — start from the revised
> hypothesis below, not from scratch.

## Original hypothesis
If the player uncovers a cataclysm's truth by gathering and combining concrete,
**systemic** clues (not lore text), they will feel an **earned "aha!"** — confirmed if
they form and voice a theory *before* the game confirms it, and report the convergence
as satisfying rather than "being told."

## What happened
PARTIALLY CONFIRMED. The core deduction *did* land (the player concluded "an unnatural /
unusual fire"), but the play pattern was **gather-everything-then-pick** rather than a
theory forming mid-investigation. The standalone "investigate → choose a conclusion"
structure turned discovery into a **menu choice at the end** — a separate deduction
minigame that **pulls the player out of the game** instead of deepening play. Verdict from
the playtester: *"I honestly didn't find this all that compelling… I have a feeling what we
just tested might pull the player out of the game mid play."*

## What to KEEP
1. **People/place-delivered discovery feels alive.** Speaking to the traveler was the most
   enjoyable beat *even though it was mechanically identical* to the environmental clues —
   the social framing made the world feel alive. **Knowledge should come from encounters
   (peoples, places), not abstract journals.**
2. **Knowledge as the reward is the right instinct** — but the reward should be tangible,
   not just an "aha."
3. The cataclysm direction (a fire that *fell* / was unnatural) reads well as lore content.

## What to CHANGE (the single most important thing)
**Cut the deduction entirely.** No puzzle, no "pick the answer." Instead:
- **Lore-as-power.** Lore fragments are gathered through normal play (venturing, expanding,
  and talking to peoples) and **assemble themselves** into the full story over time. Each
  body of knowledge grants a **thematically-matched mechanical perk** — e.g. learning of
  *the great fire* → a defensive bonus against fire (from weather or enemy attack).
  Knowledge is literally power; discovery **pulls the player forward** into exploration
  instead of out of the game.
- **Faction-gated social sources.** Some lore comes from people, and access is conditioned
  on who they are — you can speak with **wanderers**, but **not soldiers / barbarians**.
  Who will talk to you (and what they know) is part of the systemic, reactive world.

## Revised hypothesis (for the next prototype)
*If the player gathers lore fragments organically while venturing/expanding — including from
**receptive peoples** they can choose to approach (wanderers yes, soldiers/barbarians no) —
and each completed body of knowledge grants a **thematically-matched mechanical perk** (e.g.
"the great fire" lore → fire defense), then discovery will **pull the player forward into
exploration and feel rewarding**, with no deduction step. We'll know it held if the player
**seeks out lore sources for the reward/story** and reports that it **deepened rather than
interrupted** play.*

**Riskiest assumption to test first:** that knowledge-tied-to-mechanical-reward (not a
puzzle, not pure story) is what makes discovery compelling and forward-pulling.

**Suggested next step:** `/prototype` the lore-as-power loop — ideally a small *engine*
slice wired into a minimal venture/expand action so discovery is felt *inside* play, not as
a standalone screen. (This time, feel-in-context is the point, so HTML's isolation is a
weakness.)
